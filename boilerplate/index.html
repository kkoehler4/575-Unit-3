Who's Ready for Kindergarten?

Vermont's public education system is currently ranked 3rd in the country by Education Weekly. But not all kids are equally prepared to enter the education system.

In this coordinated visualization, explore the discrepancies in Kindergarten readiness for six demographics in 53 Supervisory Unions in Vermont. Scroll to learn more about how Kindergarten readiness is measured, what the results mean for students, and the future of public education in Vermont.
*R4K = "Ready for Kindergarten"

How is Readiness Measured?

The R4K!S is not a direct assessment of students, but the observations from teachers over the first few weeks of school. The teacher assesses each student based on six dimensions of readiness: social and emotional development, communication, physical health, cognitive development, knowledge, and approaches to learning.


Teachers assess the students based on these dimensions using a four-level scale to calculate overall readiness. Students whose “Total Scores” fall into categories three and four (“Ready and Practicing” and “Ready and Performing Independently”) are identified as ready.

Vermont’s concept of school readiness is interactional: students need to be ready for schools, and schools need to be ready to accommodate the diverse needs of every child.

What does this mean for Vermont students?

Overall, 86.24% of Vermont students were deemed ready for Kindergarten in 2021. 8% more girls were ready for Kindergarten than boys, 12% more kids not elegible for free and reduced lunch were ready than kids elegible for free and reduced lunch, and 6% more kids who attended publicly funded pre-k were ready than kids who did not attend private pre-k.

This visualization can provide important insight for supervisory unions into the varying needs of different groups of students.

What is the future of public education in Vermont?

Vermont is facing an education crisis. The state's student population has dropped by 21,000 since 1997 and continues to decline. At the same time, per-student spending has increased and the staff to student ratio has stayed the same, causing an increase in property taxes. Small, rural schools, some with less than 50 kids across 6 grades, are forced to close and consolidate. With this school consolidation comes the loss of close communities, but also more opportunities for kids to make friends and connections, a conflict that has divided parents and community members in Vermont in recent years.

How Vermont schools meet the needs of kids entering Kindergarten may change as the structure of public education in Vermont also changes, with varying consequences for different demographics.
